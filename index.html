About

Why I Built SSG Advisory

For founders and service businesses that are good at what they do, but cannot keep carrying everything themselves.

I built SSG Advisory because I got tired of watching talented founders and service leaders burn out doing work that better systems, and in some cases AI, could take off their plate.

Before starting SSG Advisory, I spent over nine years inside enterprise SaaS, managing strategic accounts, building Customer Success functions from scratch, rescuing at-risk clients, and helping businesses turn expensive software into measurable outcomes.

That career gave me something many AI consultants do not have. I have been on the client side of the table. I know what it feels like to adopt new tech under pressure, defend ROI, and be the person responsible when something does not deliver.

SSG Advisory exists for what I call the Invisible Expert, consultants, coaches, and service businesses whose expertise is clear, but whose visibility, sales, and operations cannot always keep up. Every system I recommend has been tested in my own business first.

AI should feel like practical support, not another bet-the-company decision.

CS Function Design · The Access Group & NuEnergy.ai

Building CS Functions That Scale

At The Access Group, I helped shape the CS function from the ground up — joining a team of 8 where no formal CS practice existed. As the team grew to 150+, inconsistency became the problem: new hires ramped at different speeds, account plans varied by person, and there was no shared standard for reviews or documentation. I stopped the reactive cycle and built one simple structure the whole team could use — a shared success plan template, a standard QBR format, and a clear Salesforce documentation guide, supported by walkthroughs and office hours. New hires ramped faster, handoffs improved, and leadership could trust the data again. Later, at NuEnergy.ai, I built a CS function from scratch as the first CS hire — delivering 100% retention on a $2.2M Government of Canada account.
